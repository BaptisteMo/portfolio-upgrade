## Executive summary

Since March 2024 (mission still ongoing), I’ve been supporting the Klub! team through a progressive product overhaul: modernizing the UI, strengthening UX consistency, and putting in place a design method compatible with an already-committed roadmap.

Overall, we shipped **5 major workstreams** — including MyDocs, Access Request, Entry inspection, as well as operational flows (opening / hand-back) and more administrative capabilities.

## Build a product vision

For a redesign to work, we first needed to deconstruct the existing product: understand why it wasn’t being adopted and where the real friction points were.

With a newly formed product team, almost no documentation, and users that were hard to mobilize, this was the first challenge to solve.

### Reconnect with the field

At that stage, the best starting point was to talk to our **core users**: the **mall teams**. In theory, they create content and keep the retailer community active within each mall.

After a few interviews, the verbatims were unmistakable.

### Clarify the goals

These interviews helped us build clear **personas**, later used as reference points to prioritize and arbitrate workstreams.

This step was **crucial** to align the project team around a shared understanding of the stakes and field pain points.

From there, the objective became clear:

**How do we increase adoption of Klub! among mall managers and encourage them to make it the primary communication channel with their retailers?**

### Target high-impact workstreams

With a partially constrained roadmap, our approach was to focus effort on journeys that could maximize adoption and reduce operational friction — then **capitalize** on what works.

- **MyDocs**: a “pilot” initiative to validate UX choices quickly with the field and stabilize a reusable UI base.
- **Access Request**: a critical journey (compliance + operations) that re-anchored design decisions in real mall processes.
- **Entry inspection / Handover**: a foundational set of operational journeys, designed to be **replicable**.

Building on this foundation, we were able to ship faster and more consistently: **Opening** and **Hand back** were built as extensions of **Handover**, replicating validated patterns (navigation, forms, steps, validations) rather than starting from scratch.

Finally, we also handled **admin** capabilities (user management, mall configuration, access rights…), plus cross-cutting elements like navigation — less “demo-friendly”, but essential to make execution reliable day to day and keep the product scalable.

## Set standards

Quick context: when I joined, the engineering team was fully **outsourced** and based in Nantes.

Up to that point, the agency covered both development and part of the design: the technical project lead produced mockups, then presented them regularly to the project team to prep sprints.

### Establish a shared source of truth

One of my first levers was to quickly replace PowerPoint mockups with a single source of truth in **Figma**.

I introduced a weekly review within an existing ritual, which accelerated alignment and decision-making: business and tech could comment directly in the right place, with the right level of detail.

### New UI and debt reduction

The app needed a real refresh. Beyond an outdated look, several elements weren’t aligned with accessibility best practices, and some components existed in duplicate with different styles.

Step by step, we introduced new components through releases, with an immediate effect noticeable by users:

> “This is much nicer to use! When are you doing the rest?”  
> *("Oh, c’est bien plus agréable comme app ! Vous allez faire le reste quand ?")*

These improvements, combined with successive redesigns, helped us reach a more consistent interface and a more homogeneous experience across features.

### Ensure quality before release

Business teams played a key role in functional UAT. In parallel, we needed to guarantee UI/UX compliance: making sure the shipped interface matched approved designs.

I therefore actively participated in UAT phases to identify and correct gaps (UI details, behaviors, component consistency).

We also introduced a dedicated KPI — **#DesignToDev** — to measure the time spent correcting gaps between design intent and the final production UI.

## Industrialize delivery

### Turn intent into a UI Kit

To increase velocity and avoid reinventing the wheel, I built a [Figma UI Kit](/en/projects/design-system) that now includes **36 documented components**, reusable across all IT projects.

### Standardize patterns

After several cycles of design and field validation, we had a solid foundation to standardize patterns.

I capitalized on what worked: reusing complete flows from one feature to another, aligning navigation systems, and generalizing validated components.

Goal: ship faster, with more consistency, and less friction for users.

## Impact

### What users say

In several ways, we observed a positive impact after the redesigns. Email feedback was particularly encouraging after launching some key features.

The support team also shared feedback consistent with these improvements on the affected journeys.

### A few numbers

We’re currently integrating analytics tools to track feature usage and adoption more reliably.

Improvements in how we work (documentation, rituals, an increasingly complete [UI Kit](/en/projects/design-system)) helped us move from an average **DesignToDev** of around **3.5 days** in early sprints down to **0.5 day**.
