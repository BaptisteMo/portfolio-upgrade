## Context

**Klépierre** is Europe’s leading shopping-center operator, present in 10 countries with more than 70 centers.  
**Klub!** is a **B2B mobile app** designed to streamline relationships with retailers. It is the main communication channel between Klépierre, on-site center teams, and retailers: news, events, administrative tasks, and community interactions.

When I joined **Klépierre Digital Factory**, the app already had a packed roadmap, but product design had been pushed to the background. My mission was to **bring design processes back to the heart of the project**: understand real usage, structure collaboration, and lay the foundations for a durable design system.

<Callout type="tip" title="Primary goal">
Reintroduce product design as an efficiency lever (coherence, guidance, integration quality) within an already-live B2B product with an already-locked roadmap.
</Callout>

### Team setup

- 1 Product Owner + me (Digital Factory)
- 2 business representatives (another Klépierre entity)
- 1 technical PM & 2 developers (external agency)

### Deliverables

- High-fi mockups
- Prototypes
- Strategic support (prioritization, decision-making, alignment)
- Development follow-up (handoff, QA, acceptance support)

## Research

Early interviews and tests quickly highlighted recurring pain points:

1. Only **a few features are heavily used** because of contractual obligations — and they are hard to use
2. **Flows lack fluidity**, with a confusing information hierarchy
3. Users need more **guidance and clarity** to act with confidence

These findings led us to define **three core personas**, which helped refocus design efforts on the most critical journeys and tailor interfaces to each persona’s goals.

<Callout type="info" title="Key decision">
We anchored prioritization and design choices around **3 personas**, used continuously to arbitrate tradeoffs and validate solutions.
</Callout>

## Process

To support the transformation while keeping delivery moving, I proposed a pragmatic approach:

- Scoping sessions with the product team to set priorities
- Gathering **more direct user feedback** with fewer intermediaries
- Sharing **regular iterations** in weekly meetings and turning support + business inputs into actionable improvements
- **Optimizing the existing UI** with quick wins (hierarchy, micro-interactions, readability) to demonstrate design value fast
- Preparing **structural redesigns** (navigation + core features: document storage, access requests, user management)

<Callout type="tip" title="Why it worked">
This framework created a stronger dialogue between business and engineering and brought product design back to the center of a team historically focused on delivery.
</Callout>

## Solution

We started redesign work with one rule: **never lose sight of the three key insights identified during research.**

### 1) Redesigning a core feature: MyDocs

MyDocs initially allowed center managers to share documents with store managers.  
Based on interviews and field observations, we expanded the feature so center managers could also find and manage **legal documents** (leases, invoices, etc.).

What we did:

- Interviews + field observations to understand real usage
- Feature scope extension based on observed needs
- Multiple iterations, demos, and user tests across persona representatives

Observed outcomes:

- Better readability
- Fewer unnecessary actions
- Faster understanding of key flows

#### Challenge: making Figma collaboration clearer

At first, stakeholders struggled to differentiate finished mockups from work-in-progress.  
We improved clarity with:

- Dedicated spaces per feature
- Direct links inside Jira tickets
- Splitting mockups ticket-by-ticket with the technical PM

### 2) Adding a brand-new feature: Move-in inspections

We then tackled a major initiative: integrating **move-in inspections** directly into the app.

Goals:

- Replace paper processes
- Unify processes across countries
- Prevent data loss by syncing with **Atlas**
- Capture data early and keep retail-unit status regularly updated

How we delivered it:

- Discovery phase (best practices + country prerequisites)
- Scenarios and user flows during ideation workshops
- Hi-fi mockups and prototypes tested with users
- User tests in staging environments directly in shopping centers

Notable feedback (field/pilots):

- Intuitive onboarding with minimal support needed
- Significant time saved on note-taking (tester feedback)
- High satisfaction as the feature met expectations after rollout in pilot centers

---

### 3) Reducing integration friction: #DesignToDev

As redesigns progressed, it became hard to estimate the effort tied to **front-end integration feedback** (spacing, missing shadows, incorrect typography, etc.).

I introduced a Jira metric called **#DesignToDev**:

- Flags integration-only feedback explicitly
- Makes the cost visible sprint over sprint
- Encourages better handoff, documentation, and componentization

<Callout type="tip" title="Impact">
#DesignToDev improved from an average of **3.5 days per sprint** to **0.5 day**, after iterating on handoff quality and documentation.
</Callout>

- 🧩 **A complete, well-documented UI kit**, enabling live iteration with business teams

- 📈 **Higher adoption** of redesigned features (business feedback + field observations)
- 🤝 **Stronger collaboration** between design, business, and engineering via a clear method and shared language
- 🔄 **Design system rollout** expanding across the Digital Factory
