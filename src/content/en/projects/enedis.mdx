## Executive summary

Enedis commissioned us to run a **UX framing** mission for the *Blackout* program: connect **more than 2,600 sites** via satellite and ensure service continuity in sensitive areas.

The project was accumulating delays, largely due to a simple issue: the absence of a **clear, shared tracking system**. Steering relied on **“giant” Excel files**, progressively enriched by different actors — a fragile loop, prone to errors and close to breaking point.

In **6 weeks**, my role was to **reduce uncertainty**: understand real practices, align teams on a single process, and turn that into **actionable** deliverables (priorities, recommendations, intent mockups) so development could start on a solid foundation.

## Reconstruct the data lifecycle

The first two weeks were marked by a **massive inflow of information** and one priority: bring structure.

The first step wasn’t to “design an interface”, but to understand:
- **who** produces the information,
- **where** it flows,
- **when** it changes status,
- and most importantly **why** (business rules, validations, dependencies).

One quote summed up the situation well:
> “Every time we open the Excel file, we wonder if we’re going to break everything.”

This phase established a reliable diagnosis before any recommendation: without a shared understanding of the data, it’s impossible to build the right tracking tool.

---

## Align teams on a single process

Once the material was collected, I ran several **business focus groups**: each team described “their version” of the process, from order initiation through to invoicing.

Using **FigJam**, I structured a transversal view:
- stakeholders at each step,
- tools and dependencies,
- interactions, loops, crossovers,
- friction areas and breaking points.

The key moment was a cross-team validation session to reach **one single version of the process**, shared and understood by everyone — not just a “by-silo” perspective.

## Turn it into an actionable specification

The deliverable format was open. The objective was simple: produce artifacts that **outlive the framing phase** and become a working base for what comes next (decisions, prioritization, development).

We therefore delivered three complementary layers:

- **A map** (macro + detail): interactions, dependencies, sensitive points
- **A prioritized synthesis document**: pain points, hypotheses, recommendations, possible scenarios
- **Intent mockups**: make a direction tangible, help projection, and frame early technical discussions

### Outcome

The main impact wasn’t “a pretty mockup”, but a shared decision base:

- 🔍 **Shared vision established**: a clear understanding of the end-to-end process
- 🧭 **Stable framing**: development was able to start the following year on stable foundations, with very little additional discovery needed to initiate the project
- 💬 **Business/tech alignment**: smoother exchanges, better-shared priorities
- 👍 **Very positive feedback**: teams felt heard and understood in a project that had been fragmented up to that point

---

## What I learned

This mission illustrates the power of UX in complex industrial environments: when the problem is primarily organizational and data-driven, design starts by **creating a shared reading**, not by drawing screens.

My role was to turn fragile tracking (Excel) into a clear, actionable foundation: **understand, align, prioritize, make the build possible**.
