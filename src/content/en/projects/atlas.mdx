## Executive summary

After one year on *Klub!* and the rollout of the UI kit, I joined **ATLAS**: the internal tool that centralizes the financial and contractual management of Klépierre shopping malls.

ATLAS is a **rich** product, but also **dense**, **legacy**, and **heterogeneous**: each function has its own habits, shortcuts, and workarounds. My role was to bring clarity back to it: **structure information, unify critical journeys, and re-establish design within product decision-making**.

In one year, I conducted ~**40 interviews**, ran around twenty cross-team workshops, and actively contributed to steering several transversal redesigns. Early feedback is very positive, and quantitative adoption measurements are currently being set up.

## The approach I put in place

On a complex product, the goal isn’t to “add screens”. It’s to **rebuild a shared reading** of the system, then tackle journeys that can concretely improve teams’ day-to-day work.

## Mapping a complex system

Before redesigning anything, we needed to understand **how ATLAS is actually used** — and why the same journey could be experienced very differently depending on the function.

Rather than producing a “static” map, I built an **actionable knowledge base** to stay relevant across the entire roadmap:
- around **forty interviews** with users from **6 different functions**
- around **twenty workshops** (dependencies, validations, friction areas)
- **observations** on how information moves and where handoffs happen between teams

The key methodological choice was to avoid thinking in “features”, and instead use **Jobs To Be Done**: the concrete actions users try to accomplish (find the essentials quickly, secure a step, move a deal forward, arbitrate…).

This approach surfaced transversal findings (independently of design debt) that became the guiding thread for redesigns and helped shape my product design strategy over time:
- **Steer your activity based on current priorities** (not based on the tool’s structure)
- **Access the essentials in a few seconds** (meeting, call, arbitration)
- **Find overall consistency** across features to avoid re-entry, data inconsistencies…

These insights were then used as a decision framework: leverage every initiative to improve the whole (clarify, reduce noise, make action more immediate) rather than optimizing an isolated feature.

## Creating the user cockpit

The Todo wasn’t meant to be a “to-do list”: it was supposed to be **ATLAS’s personal cockpit**, where each user drives their activity.

The paradox: the feature was **almost abandoned**. No one could clearly say “what was wrong”, precisely because it was no longer used. So we framed the redesign with a simple ambition: **recreate a workspace that’s actually usable day to day**.

Design principles:
- **Reduce visual overload** (fewer columns, clearer hierarchy)
- **Speed up repeated actions** (grouped actions, batch updates)
- **Give control back to users** (advanced filters, customization)

Several iteration cycles with the business functions helped balance density and readability, and collectively validate journey understanding.

## Unifying the contract workflow (Speed of Execution)

ATLAS’s most structuring KPI is tied to its main workflow: a process of around **15 steps**. The indicator monitored by the business is **“Speed of Execution”**: teams’ ability to move a case forward efficiently, from offer validation to the contractual integration that enables billing to start.

Leadership requested improvements to **steps 4 to 9**, a critical segment of the journey (coordination between legal and leasing teams, validation workflow, information quality).

### Methodology:
- **1:1 interviews** to clarify roles and responsibilities
- **Cross-team focus groups** to align needs and shared reference points
- **End-to-end mapping** (user flows + dependencies)
- **Mockups & prototypes** to test a more unified experience and anticipate transversal needs (e.g., a future notifications system)

### What we put in place

Based on this feedback, we converged on **four topics** that address the major issues and now serve as the guiding principles for the 2026 roadmap:

- **Capitalize on the new Todo** to manage the various actions to be carried out throughout the flow (next steps, validations, expected documents, etc.)
- **Rethink contract negotiation** where it actually takes place and maintain the partnerships between leasing team members, allowing them to do their work in an existing tool: a **Word add-in** connected to ATLAS to manage the legal validation workflow
- **Create a control step** to quickly verify that the contract data and ATLAS data are consistent before moving forward with the deal and sending everything for signature, ensuring that decisions are based on the correct data
- **Implement an automation component** to reduce unnecessary exchanges and speed up the availability of legal documents once the contract is signed

To date, only the **first area (Todo)** has been **deployed**.  
The following areas (Word + control + automation) are currently **under development**.

## Impacts

- **Design is reintegrated** as a structuring step: framing, arbitration, cross-function alignment.
- **A shared reading** of pain points, enabling prioritization and product decisions.
- **Progressive standardization** (continuing the design system initiated on Klub!).
- **First redesigns shipped** with a very high satisfaction level (quantitative measures to come).
- **2025–2026 outlook**: strong willingness to continue the effort (roadmap + deeper redesign).

### Next steps

- Build the 2026 roadmap with business teams
- Continue redesigning critical workflow journeys
- Consolidate UI consistency (components + patterns)
- Strengthen quality checks (UI/UX) during pre-release QA before production
